What is Alzheimer's?

Alzheimer’s disease is a progressive brain disease that has symptoms that slowly start to interfere with memory.

-Alzheimer’s is the most common type of dementia, and usually occurs in those age 65 or older.
-But, there is also early-onset Alzheimer’s that occurs in approximately 200,000 Americans.
-The disease progresses from mild to moderate to severe, as it is a terminal illness.


Current research and findings:

While there is currently no cure for Alzheimer’s, scientists are working hard to find one.
Currently, scientists suspect that two things are damaging brain nerve cells.
-Plaques: deposits of beta-amyloid (protein) that build up in spaces between nerve cells
-Tangles: twisted fiber os tau (protein) that build up inside cells
But, scientists still don’t know how these affect Alzheimer’s disease, but they know these are common in patients.

-Currently, scientists are working on these:
Video Game to Detect Alzheimer's
Eye Protein Levels Predict Alzheimer's
Abnormal Tau Protein in Former NFL PLayers
 Artifical Intelligence Could Help Research


Facts and Figures

-Alzheimer’s brain changes may begin 20 years before symptoms appear
-Almost 6 million Americans are living with Alzheimer’s
-Alzheimer’s is the sixth leading cause of death in the United States
-Every 65 seconds someone in the United States develops the disease
-In 2019, Alzheimer’s and other dementias will cost the US 290 billion, and by 2050 these can rise to 1.1 trillion
-1 in 10 Americans over the age of 65 has alzheimer's

How to Care for Someone Living with the Disease

-Establish routines for them
-Provide simple instructions for them to understand
-Reduce distractions to make it easier to focus
-Create a safe environment for them
-Empathize and show them compassion
-Understand the disease and how they are living

Hold Fundraisers Donate or encourage others to Volunteer

Donate

1) Hold Fundraisers

As a teen, fundraisers are a good way to raise money for a cure.
You can do so at your school, community, or online.
Participating in the Longest Day, which is one of their fundraisers on June 21st, could be a way to show your support.

2) Donate

Because funding is a such a crucial part of finding a cure, donating is a way to help.
If you can't donate yourself, you can reach out and ask members of your community to.

3) Volunteer

If you don't want to give money, you can give time instead.
Volunteering at places like the Alzheimer's Association could be helpful to both them and you, as you could learn how fundraising works.
